## 1. Page Goal & Audience

### Goal
Convert visiting fire candidates into waitlist signups. One action. No competing CTAs. Every section serves the waitlist conversion.

### Audience
**Primary:** Entry-level fire candidates — anyone applying for an entry-level firefighter position, regardless of attempt number. Some are first-timers. Many are on attempt #3, #5, #10 — and arguably more motivated to find prep tools because they've felt the cost of being cut.

**Locked language pattern:** The page should acknowledge multi-attempt candidates directly somewhere in the audience-facing copy. Pattern example: *"Whether it's your first attempt or your fifth..."* This single line tells the entire audience the page is built for them, not just rookies.

**Audience demographics (working profile, not on the page):** Typically 18–40, range of backgrounds — civilian, military, EMS, volunteer fire, lateral career changers. Many have already passed CPAT, NTN, or department-specific written exams. The unifying anxiety is the oral interview — most opaque, most subjective-feeling, and highest-stakes part of the process.

**Out of scope for this page:**
- **Lateral candidates** — TBD on roadmap. Depends on (a) whether enough lateral-specific oral board questions can be sourced to build a quality bank and (b) how different lateral processes actually are from entry-level. Page does not feature lateral content yet, but copy should avoid first-timer-only language that would make lateral candidates feel excluded if they land here.
- **Promotional candidates** (engineer, lieutenant, captain, chief promotional boards) — out entirely. Not a target audience now or in the foreseeable roadmap.

**What this audience trusts:** Direct language. Fire service vocabulary used correctly. Honesty. Someone from inside the service speaking to them as a peer, not a customer.

**What this audience doesn't trust:** Generic "interview tips" articles, expensive coaching services with no transparency, pages that feel like marketing fluff written by someone who's never been in a station.

*Source: Real-world fire service hiring landscape; standard candidate persona principles (NN/g UX research personas guidance).*

## 2. Stage & Honesty Posture

The product is **pre-launch / waitlist.** This must be communicated clearly and early. Pre-launch pages that pretend to be live break trust the moment a user clicks the CTA.

**Posture for the entire page:** Confident about the vision, honest about the stage. "This is being built. Get in early. Here's what you get for being early."

This is not a weakness — it's a positioning advantage. Candidates who care enough to join a waitlist for a prep tool are exactly the candidates most likely to use it well, give feedback, and become paying users post-launch.

*Source: Standard pre-launch landing page best practices (Y Combinator Startup School; Lenny Rachitsky pre-launch waitlist guidance); honesty-as-positioning is a documented conversion driver in trust-building UX research.*

## 3. Founder & Credibility Posture

**Truth to communicate:** Built by an active firefighter who went through *many* hiring processes — across multiple departments, multiple process variations — and saw the prep gap firsthand, repeatedly.

This is a meaningfully stronger credibility position than "active firefighter who recently got hired." Multi-process candidate experience is rare and valuable. You've seen:
- How different departments structure oral boards
- What patterns repeat across processes (and what doesn't)
- What a candidate actually feels and needs at each stage
- Where prep tools fail candidates in real conditions

**Truth NOT to communicate:** Insider knowledge from sitting on oral boards as an evaluator. (You haven't.) Coaching track record. (Not yet.)

**The right framing:** *Candidate-built, by someone who navigated the path repeatedly.* Not "the firefighter who got hired and remembered the prep gap." Closer to "the firefighter who saw the gap from the inside, across multiple departments, and is building the tool he wishes existed."

**Anonymity:** Role-based credibility for now ("Built by an active firefighter") with the option to add a real name later. Worth flagging the tradeoff: pages with named founders convert better than anonymous ones, all else equal. Adding your name later will likely lift signups. Not a blocker now.

## 6. Section-by-Section Direction

### Section 1 — Hero

**Purpose:** In ~5 seconds, tell the visitor what FirePrep is, who it's for, and what to do next. Replace the "FAIL" framing with a clear promise.

**Required elements:**
- Headline: lead with the product's core promise, not the problem
- Subhead: one sentence explaining how it works and who it's for
- Pre-launch tag visible and clear ("Joining the waitlist — launching soon" or similar)
- Single CTA: "Join the Waitlist" (not "Sign Up")
- Email input + button
- Micro-copy under the form stating the offer ("Free first 90 days at launch + early access")
- Hero visual (the firefighters-at-a-burn image is good — keep it, it's earned)

**Copy direction (not final):**

Headline pattern options to brief Claude Code on:
- *"Practice the oral board the way you'll be graded on it."*
- *"Walk into your panel having already answered 50 questions — scored against real rubrics."*
- *"The oral board prep that grades you on what panels actually score."*

The pattern: **specific promise + credibility marker.** Avoid abstract words like "structured preparation" or "high-fidelity assessment." Use concrete nouns: questions, rubrics, criteria, scores, panels.

Subhead pattern:
- *"Built by an active firefighter for entry-level candidates. AI-graded practice sessions mapped to standard fire service competency frameworks."*

**What to remove:**
- "[ PROTOCOL: ACTIVE PREPARATION ]" — sounds like sci-fi gaming, not fire service
- "LIVE: AI PANEL SIMULATION READY" — false (product isn't live)
- "FAIL." as the dominant visual word

**What to keep:**
- Dark background, orange accent
- Hero image of firefighters
- Bold display typography
- Two-column hero layout (text left, image right)

*Source: NN/g — landing page hero clarity research. Flint McGlaughlin / MarketingExperiments value proposition framework: clear, exclusive, credible, useful. Headline-as-promise outperforms headline-as-problem in B2C conversion testing.*

### Section 2 — Why This Exists

**Purpose:** Establish the real problem in plain language. Frame why a tool like this needs to exist. Currently this is the "Challenge / Solution" two-column block — that structure is fine, but the copy is too abstract and the "Solution" side overclaims.

**Required elements:**
- Two-column or single-column section
- Problem side: 2–3 short paragraphs naming what's actually broken about how candidates prepare
- Solution side: 2–3 short paragraphs naming what FirePrep does differently — *described as what it does, not as marketing claims*

**Copy direction (not final):**

Problem-side substance to convey:
- Most candidates prepare by reading lists of "common questions" online and rehearsing answers in their head or in front of a mirror
- They don't know what panels actually score on, in what order, with what weights
- Real feedback only comes during the actual interview — when it's too late to use it
- The gap between "I prepared" and "I prepared *correctly*" is where careers are lost

Solution-side substance to convey:
- Practice questions built around the same competency areas oral boards evaluate (composure under pressure, decision-making, communication, integrity, fire service knowledge, etc.)
- Each answer scored against a structured rubric — see exactly which criteria you hit and which you missed
- Feedback delivered immediately, not after the fact
- Built around how panels actually evaluate, not generic "interview tips"

**Critical rewrite:**
- Drop "Responses mapped directly to official departmental rubrics" — this overclaims and isn't legally clean. Replace with: "Questions and rubrics built around standard fire service competency frameworks (NFPA 1001, NTN-style oral board evaluation)."
- Drop "high-fidelity assessment" — vague marketing language. Replace with concrete description of what the AI actually does.

*Source: OPM Structured Interview Guide (2008) — competency-based interviewing methodology; Latham et al. (1980) — Behavioral Observation Scales, foundation for criterion-based scoring; NFPA 1001 — Firefighter Professional Qualifications standard.*

### Section 3 — Product Preview (NEW — replaces "FINAL OBSTACLE" image break)

**Purpose:** Show the product. Currently the page has zero visual proof of what FirePrep actually is — only marketing copy about it. This is the biggest contributor to the "AI-generated" feeling.

**Required elements:** Pick one of the following based on what's actually buildable:

**Option A (strongest if available):** A real or mocked-up screenshot of a graded answer — showing the question, an example transcript, the rubric criteria, the score per criterion, and the AI feedback. Even a static mockup is dramatically more convincing than no preview.

**Option B (if no UI exists yet):** A "What you'll get" visual — a clean graphic showing the structure of a session: 5–10 questions → AI grading → criterion-level feedback → competency score over time. Visual flow, not screenshots.

**Option C (weakest but acceptable):** A worked example written out — show one real practice question, what a strong vs. weak answer looks like, and what the rubric scoring would say. Long-form but proves the substance.

**Recommendation:** Option A. If you don't have UI yet, build a simple static mockup specifically for this page. The proof-of-product is worth the time.

**What to cut:** The current "THE INTERVIEW IS THE FINAL OBSTACLE" section. It's visually striking but adds no information — it just restates the hero theme with different words. Real estate that prominent should be earning its place.

*Source: Baymard Institute — product visualization on pre-launch and SaaS landing pages is a top conversion lever. NN/g — "show, don't tell" principle in product UX communication.*

### Section 4 — Built for Entry-Level Candidates (First Attempt or Fifth)

**Purpose:** Speak directly to entry-level candidates across the full attempt range — first-timers AND multi-attempt candidates. Currently the page splits this with Lateral & Promotional, which dilutes the message and confuses the audience.

**Decision:** Cut Lateral & Promotional column entirely. Single-audience focus on entry-level. (Lateral content can come back as a separate page or future expansion when sourcing and process-fit are validated. Promotional is permanently out of scope for this product line.)

**Required elements:**
- Section header naming the audience clearly — should explicitly cover both first-time and multi-attempt candidates
- 3–5 bullet points or short paragraphs that name specific entry-level pain points and how FirePrep addresses each
- At least one piece of copy that speaks to multi-attempt candidates directly
- Tone: peer-to-peer, not patronizing

**Copy direction (not final):**

**Header pattern:** *"Built for entry-level candidates — whether it's your first attempt or your fifth."*

This single line is critical. It signals to the multi-attempt candidate that the page sees them, and to the first-timer that they're not alone. Both audiences self-include without one being prioritized.

The current entry-level copy is decent — keep its substance, refine the language:
- "You possess skill, but no framework for situational oral board responses." → keep, sharpen
- "You are walking into a high-pressure environment without a mechanism to simulate stress." → keep, but tighten
- "You need to build confidence that only comes from repeated, graded practice." → keep, this one is good

Add bullets that hit specific entry-level realities, including multi-attempt:
- The gap between knowing fire service concepts and being able to articulate them under pressure
- Most prep advice online is generic — you need fire-specific
- **For multi-attempt candidates:** *"You've done this before. You know what it costs to fall short. The next attempt should be the last."* (Or similar — language is direction, not final.)

**Tradeoff to name:** Cutting Lateral content means lateral candidates who land on the page may bounce. That's the cost. The benefit is entry-level candidates feel the page is built specifically for them. Single-audience focus consistently outperforms split-audience pages in conversion testing.

**Lateral consideration:** Avoid first-timer-only language ("your first time facing the panel," "before you ever sit in front of an evaluator") — multi-attempt entry-level candidates already have, and lateral candidates definitely have. Use language that works for *anyone preparing for an entry-level oral board.*

*Source: NN/g — message-audience match on landing pages. ConversionXL / CXL Institute — single-audience focus as a documented conversion driver.*

### Section 5 — Founder Section (NEW)

**Purpose:** Establish credibility through who built this, not what it claims. This is the single highest-impact addition to the page.

**Required elements:**
- Section header that signals credibility ("Why I'm building this," "Built by a firefighter," or similar)
- 2–4 short paragraphs in first person, role-based for now (no real name yet, per your decision)
- A photo if possible — even a silhouette or gear shot, not necessarily a face. Something visual that signals "real person, real fire service." If no photo, this section is still valuable, just less powerful.
- A statement of intent or honesty — what you're building, why, what you're not claiming

**Copy direction (not final):**

Substance to convey, in first person:

- I'm an active firefighter. I went through *many* hiring processes across multiple departments before getting on the job — and I saw the prep gap from the inside, repeatedly.
- The advice online is generic. The expensive coaching options are opaque. The actual scoring criteria panels use are rooted in published frameworks — but no tool actually uses those frameworks to grade your practice.
- I'm not an oral board evaluator. I haven't sat on a panel. What I have is the candidate's perspective — across multiple processes, multiple departments, and the kind of pattern recognition you only get from going through it more than once.
- This is being built openly. Waitlist members get input on what gets built and what changes. The goal is to make the prep tool I wish I'd had on attempt one.

**Critical:** Do not imply you've sat on oral boards. Do not imply insider knowledge of how panels score. The credibility is *candidate-built, multi-process, fire-service-grounded* — not *evaluator-insider.* This is more honest, and the right kind of candidate will respect it more than the alternative.

**The "many processes" detail matters.** It distinguishes you from the generic "active firefighter built this" framing and signals real, repeated, candidate-side expertise. It also speaks directly to multi-attempt candidates reading the page — they see themselves in the founder's story.

*Source: Honesty-as-positioning: Basecamp, 37signals, and Y Combinator pre-launch guidance all point to founder honesty as a stronger trust signal than polished marketing. Trust signals research: NN/g, Baymard Institute.*

### Section 6 — How It Works (Replaces "GET HIRED" Initiation/Optimization/Elite Result)

**Purpose:** Show the prep process in plain, non-jargon language.

**Problem with current section:** "INITIATION → OPTIMIZATION → ELITE RESULT" sounds like sci-fi gaming, not fire service hiring. "Application → FirePrep → Hired" is the actual structure but the labels obscure it. Candidates don't think of themselves as going through "Initiation" — they think of themselves as practicing for an interview.

**Required elements:**
- 3 steps, clearly numbered
- Each step in plain language — what happens, what the candidate gets out of it
- Visual or layout that makes the progression clear (left-to-right or top-to-bottom)

**Copy direction (not final):**

Step structure to convey:

1. **Practice realistic oral board questions.** 5–10 questions per session, drawn from a bank built around standard fire service competencies. Speak your answer out loud — recorded, transcribed, ready to grade.

2. **Get scored against a real rubric.** Every answer evaluated against 4–6 criteria — the same categories panels use (composure, decision-making, communication, integrity, etc.). See exactly which criteria you hit, which you missed, with specific feedback per criterion.

3. **Track your weak spots over time.** Competency scores roll up across sessions — see where you're consistently strong and where you keep losing points. Practice deliberately. Walk into the real panel knowing what you've fixed.

**Visual direction:** Three numbered steps, generous spacing, clean iconography. The current "GET HIRED" giant background text is dramatic but it's positioning, not information. Keep dramatic, drop unnecessary.

*Source: NN/g — "How it works" sections perform best when they describe concrete user actions, not abstract product benefits.*

- **Language register:** Cut all sci-fi / gaming framing ("PROTOCOL," "SYSTEM READY," "INITIATION," "ELITE RESULT," version numbers in footer). Replace with fire-service-appropriate plain language.
- **Section transitions:** Add cleaner visual separation between sections so the eye knows when one finishes and another begins.

**What gets added:**
- Founder section (new, needs visual treatment that matches the rest)
- Product preview section (new, treatment depends on which preview option is chosen)
- Multi-attempt acknowledgment language treatment (likely a subhead or callout)

**Implementation note for Claude Code:** Don't redesign — refine. The current Tailwind setup likely already has a usable color palette and typography scale. Audit what's there, fix inconsistencies, fill gaps. Don't introduce new design tokens unless they solve a real problem.
